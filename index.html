The Data Behind the Shift

The Core Insight:
When we look at San Francisco over the last twenty years, we often talk about the skyscrapers and the tech companies that redefined the skyline. But as we dug into two decades of police data—over 2.5 million individual records—we found a different story. It isn't just a story of crime going up or down; it’s a story of crime moving.

As the city grew wealthier and more crowded, the "footprint" of where things happen changed. Our analysis shows that while some neighborhoods seem quieter than they were twenty years ago, the energy hasn't disappeared. It has been squeezed, pushed along the edges of the city, and concentrated into new hubs. We aren't necessarily looking at a "safer" map; we are looking at a map where the borders of the city's problems have been shifted.

A Tale of Two Eras
Imagine walking through San Francisco in 2003. You’d see the gritty industrial warehouses of the neighborhood SoMa and the affordable, lived-in blocks of the Mission. Now, fast-forward to 2023. Those same streets are now dominated by the Salesforce Tower, high-end boutiques, and artisanal cafes.

We are going to walk you through three specific views:

The Change: How the actual types of incidents shifted as the city got wealthier.
The Squeeze:A look at how the "hotspots" on our map were pushed out of the center and toward the edges.
The Reality: A look at how the city’s ability to handle these incidents has changed, even as the city’s wealth exploded.

By the end, you’ll see that the "safe" streets we see today might just be the result of moving the problem a few blocks over—out of sight, but still very much in the data.
