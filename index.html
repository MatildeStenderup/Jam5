The Data Behind the Shift

When we look at San Francisco over the last twenty years, we often talk about the skyscrapers and the tech companies that redefined the skyline. But as we dug into two decades of police data—over 2.5 million individual records—we found a different story. It isn't just a story of crime going up or down; it’s a story of crime moving.

A Tale of Two Eras
Imagine walking through San Francisco in 2003. You’d see the gritty industrial warehouses of the neighborhood SoMa and the affordable, lived-in blocks of the Mission. Now, fast-forward to 2023. Those same streets are now dominated by the Salesforce Tower, high-end boutiques, and artisanal cafes.

As the city grew wealthier and more crowded, the "footprint" of where things happen changed. Our analysis shows that while some neighborhoods seem quieter than they were twenty years ago, the energy hasn't disappeared. It has been squeezed, pushed along the edges of the city, and concentrated into new hubs. We aren't necessarily looking at a "safer" map; we are looking at a map where the borders of the city's problems have been shifted.

The Migrating Map: Tracking San Francisco’s Shifting Landscape

The Plot Analysis
When we look at these two plots together, the data tells a story of a city that has effectively "squeezed" its challenges into a smaller territory.

On the left, the boxplot shows that the extreme high-volume years of 2003 are gone; the citywide average has dropped. But the radar chart on the right shows that this "improvement" wasn't shared by everyone. In 2003, the Mission was the clear peak of the map. By 2023, the Mission and Southern districts—the two areas most transformed by the tech-driven real estate boom—have seen their incident volumes recede significantly.

The most striking part of the data is where the map expands. The Central and Tenderloin districts stand out as the only two neighborhoods where the orange 2023 line pushes past the 2003 era. As the "tech boom" redefined the blocks around them, the Tenderloin and Central sectors became the new epicenters. We aren't looking at a city where crime is simply disappearing; we are looking at a city where the problems have been pushed inward, creating a more intense concentration in the city's commercial heart.

Moving Beyond the Map
But knowing where the incidents have concentrated is only the first layer. If the Central and Tenderloin districts are the only ones seeing an increase, we have to ask: What is happening there?
Is this increase driven by the same types of incidents we saw twenty years ago, or has the "flavor" of the city's problems evolved? To understand the reality behind the "Tech Boom" era, we need to break these shapes down into the specific categories of these incidents.
