The Shifting Border: Did San Francisco Fix Crime or Just Move It?

A data-driven exploration of crime patterns in San Francisco from 2003 to 2023.

The Data Behind the Shift

When we look at San Francisco over the last twenty years, we often talk about the skyscrapers and the tech companies that redefined the skyline. But as we dug into two decades of police data—over 2.5 million individual records—we found a different story. It isn't just a story of crime going up or down; it’s a story of crime moving.

A Tale of Two Eras
Imagine walking through San Francisco in 2003. You’d see the gritty industrial warehouses of the neighborhood SoMa and the affordable, lived-in blocks of the Mission. Now, fast-forward to 2023. Those same streets are now dominated by the Salesforce Tower, high-end boutiques, and artisanal cafes.

As the city grew wealthier and more crowded, the "footprint" of where things happen changed. Our analysis shows that while some neighborhoods seem quieter than they were twenty years ago, the energy hasn't disappeared. It has been squeezed, pushed along the edges of the city, and concentrated into new hubs. We aren't necessarily looking at a "safer" map; we are looking at a map where the borders of the city's problems have been shifted.

The Migrating Map: Tracking San Francisco’s Shifting Landscape

The Plot Analysis
When we look at these two plots together, the data tells a story of a city that has effectively "squeezed" its challenges into a smaller territory.

On the left, the boxplot shows that the extreme high-volume years of 2003 are gone; the citywide average has dropped. But the radar chart on the right shows that this "improvement" wasn't shared by everyone. In 2003, the Mission was the clear peak of the map. By 2023, the Mission and Southern districts—the two areas most transformed by the tech-driven real estate boom—have seen their incident volumes recede significantly.

The most striking part of the data is where the map expands. The Central and Tenderloin districts stand out as the only two neighborhoods where the orange 2023 line pushes past the 2003 era. As the "tech boom" redefined the blocks around them, the Tenderloin and Central sectors became the new epicenters. We aren't looking at a city where crime is simply disappearing; we are looking at a city where the problems have been pushed inward, creating a more intense concentration in the city's commercial heart.

Moving Beyond the Map
But knowing where the incidents have concentrated is only the first layer. If the Central and Tenderloin districts are the only ones seeing an increase, we have to ask: What is happening there?
Is this increase driven by the same types of incidents we saw twenty years ago, or has the "flavor" of the city's problems evolved? To understand the reality behind the "Tech Boom" era, we need to break these shapes down into the specific categories of these incidents.

Two Decades of Shifting Incidents and Social Isolation

The Choropleth maps with Plotly Analysis

The interactive maps beside display each district’s share of citywide incidents. If you hovering over the map, you can see the top three incident types that dominated these areas in 2003 versus 2023. When we have a closer look, we see that the "flavor" of San Francisco’s districts conditions didn't just move it also transformed a bit.

As we noted above, the crimes of Central district has grown bigger. In 2003, Central saw a significant number of larceny and theft cases (around 3,400). Fast forward to 2023, and that number hasn't just grown, it has exploded. Larceny and theft increase to over 7,700 incidents, now dominating the district’s data. This suggests that as the city’s centrum grew more affluent, it became a massive magnet for property crime.

Meanwhile Tenderloin tells a sad consistency. In both 2003 and 2023, the primary driver of police activity remained the same, drug and narcotic offenses. However, a troubling new crime type have emerged in the top three for 2023, Assault. As the surrounding neighborhoods were reshaped by affluent, tourism and the tech boom, Tenderloin became more socially isolated. The data shows that rather than being "cleaned up", the city's substance abuse and safety challenges have been concentrated into this small sector, turning it into a "dumping ground" where social problems are pushed and held.

The news is different for the Southern and Mission districts.Which once where hubs for larceny/theft and other offenses in 2003, their crime shares have plummeted especially Southern district. The arrival of tech headquarters, nice restaurants and a shifting demographic has effectively displaced the older patterns of crime.

Looking at these twenty years of data, the patterns reveal an interesting shift in crime types across the city. San Francisco hasn't solved its problems, it has categorized them. Property crimes have flooded the tourist and retail zones of the Northern and Central districts, while drug and behavioral crises have been locked firmly into Tenderloin.

Dissecting the Shift in Crime Types

Figure 3, the interactive bar chart, allows for a granular look at the top five crime categories within each district, comparing 2003 and 2023. This visualization is crucial for understanding how the nature of incidents has evolved alongside their geographical shift.

As the previous maps indicated, the Tenderloin and Central districts are the only areas where overall incident counts increased. Drilling into the interactive chart for the Tenderloin reveals a troubling consistency: "DRUG/NARCOTIC" offenses remain a dominant category in both 2003 and 2023, and have even seen an increase in raw numbers. Furthermore, "ASSAULT" emerges as a top category in 2023, suggesting a potential intensification of social issues within this concentrated area. This reinforces the idea that the district has become a 'dumping ground' where social problems are pushed rather than solved, potentially exacerbated by lower average income levels which can correlate with higher rates of substance abuse and interpersonal violence.

In contrast, the Central district shows a dramatic rise in "LARCENY/THEFT" incidents. In 2003, while present, it was not as overwhelming as in 2023. This surge in property crime aligns with the district's transformation into a more affluent commercial and tourist hub. The increased presence of valuable targets, coupled with a higher density of people and an influx of higher-income residents and visitors, creates more opportunities for such offenses, suggesting a shift from crimes of necessity to crimes of opportunity targeting wealth.
For districts like Mission and Southern, which saw a decrease in overall incidents, the interactive chart often shows a reduction across most categories. "LARCENY/THEFT" and "ASSAULT" often remain present but at significantly lower volumes, reflecting the broader displacement effect discussed earlier. The changing demographics and economic landscape in these areas, including significant gentrification and a rise in median incomes, likely contributed to these shifts as older, less affluent populations and associated crime patterns were pushed out.

This detailed view confirms that San Francisco's crime landscape has not merely shifted location but has also reconfigured its very character, heavily influenced by the city's evolving socioeconomic stratification. The city's problems have become more specialized: property crimes in its commercially vibrant and high-income core, and concentrated social and drug-related issues in its historically vulnerable, lower-income neighborhoods.




The Future of San Francisco's Urban Fabric: A Call to Action

The data unequivocally demonstrates that while crime rates may shift, the underlying social and economic factors driving these patterns persist and adapt. San Francisco has not eradicated its challenges but rather redistributed them, concentrating specific issues in particular areas. This analysis is not merely an academic exercise; it's a stark illustration of how urban development, economic disparity, and policy choices directly impact the safety and well-being of its residents.

Moving forward, a truly effective approach to public safety requires acknowledging these complex interdependencies. It means shifting from reactive policing to proactive, community-based solutions that address root causes. Investments in social services, affordable housing, mental health support, and economic opportunities in historically marginalized districts are not just social welfare initiatives; they are critical components of a comprehensive crime reduction strategy. For the city to truly thrive, it must confront the socio-economic divisions laid bare by this data and work towards an inclusive urban future where prosperity is shared, and safety is a reality for all its communities.
