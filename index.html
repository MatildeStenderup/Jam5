The Data Behind the Shift

The Core Insight:
The story of San Francisco over the last twenty years is often told through the lens of a "Tech Boom," but the police data tells a more complicated story of displacement. As once-industrial districts like SoMa and working-class hubs like The Mission transformed into high-rent corridors, the "footprint" of criminal incidents didn't simply shrink—it migrated. Our analysis reveals that while "street-level" crime appears to vanish from newly gentrified blocks, it often reappears with higher density in the city's remaining low-income pockets and outer-edge neighborhoods. We aren't necessarily seeing a "safer" city; we are seeing a city where the "border" of high-incident zones has been squeezed and pushed along economic fault lines. The Narrative: A Tale of Two Maps Imagine two maps of San Francisco laid on top of one another. The first, from 2003, shows a city of gritty industrial warehouses in SoMa and affordable Victorian flats in the Mission. The second, from 2024, shows a skyline dominated by the Salesforce Tower and a streetscape of high-end boutiques and artisanal cafes. In this data story, we merge two decades of SFPD incident reports—over 2.5 million records—to track the "heartbeat" of the city. We look past the simple headlines to ask a difficult question: When a neighborhood gentrifies, does the crime actually go away? We will guide you through three specific lenses: The Evolution: How the types of crime shifted as the city got wealthier (from person-to-person robberies to car break-ins). The Squeeze: A geographic look at how incident "hotspots" were pushed out of the tech core and into the periphery. The Gap: An interactive look at how our ability to resolve these crimes has changed even as the city’s wealth has exploded. By the end, you’ll see that the "safe" streets of modern San Francisco might just be the result of moving the problem a few blocks over—out of sight, but not out of the data.
