The Data Behind the Shift

The Core Insight:
When we look at San Francisco over the last twenty years, we often talk about the skyscrapers and the tech companies that redefined the skyline. But as we dug into two decades of police data—over 2.5 million individual records—we found a different story. It isn't just a story of crime going up or down; it’s a story of crime moving.

As the city grew wealthier and more crowded, the "footprint" of where things happen changed. Our analysis shows that while some neighborhoods seem quieter than they were twenty years ago, the energy hasn't disappeared. It has been squeezed, pushed along the edges of the city, and concentrated into new hubs.

A Tale of Two Eras
Imagine walking through San Francisco in 2003. You’d see the gritty industrial warehouses of SoMa and the lived-in blocks of the Mission. Now, fast-forward to 2023. Those same streets are now dominated by the Salesforce Tower and high-end boutiques.

When Neighborhoods Change, Data Moves

On the left, the boxplot shows us that San Francisco hasn't "fixed" the volume of incidents; the 2023 range sits almost exactly where 2003 did. However, the radar chart on the right explains the "squeeze."

You can see the blue area of 2003 being "pulled" as the Mission gentrified. That energy migrated: the massive orange spike in the Southern and Tenderloin districts shows where activity has concentrated. We are seeing a map where the "border" of high-incident zones has been pushed along economic lines.

Violent vs. Property

Patterns reveal interesting shifts in crime types across the city. As wealth increased, the nature of reported incidents shifted from personal safety concerns to property-based incidents.
